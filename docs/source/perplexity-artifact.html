What Kopis actually does

The real strategic thesis

Payroll-linked repayment is strategically valuable because it collapses three of the most painful variables in unsecured lending at once: default risk, collection cost, and underwriting uncertainty. When repayment happens at the wage level — before money reaches the borrower's checking account — the lender's economics change meaningfully enough to justify a new rail.

But the market already knows this. Kashable, BMG, Salary Finance, TrueConnect, OneBlinc and Perpay have all built lending products around it. Pinwheel, Argyle and Atomic have built the connectivity layer. Highline is building adjacent repayment-rail infrastructure. The category is not empty.

What is empty: a standardized, neutral, multi-lender orchestration layer that any lender can integrate once and reach any payroll system with compliant, continuity-aware, borrower-consented repayment. That's the gap Kopis can own.

Market map: the four layers

Employer lenders
Kashable, BMG/LoansAtWork, TrueConnect, Salary Finance, OneBlinc — originate and collect payroll-linked loans through employer distribution.
Payroll APIs
Pinwheel, Argyle, Atomic — connect to payroll systems and enable direct deposit switching, income/employment verification, paycheck-linked flows.
Repayment rails
Highline, Perpay-style mechanics — move money on paycheck cycles, often for a specific use case.
Lender-agnostic orchestration
Open. Nobody cleanly productizes all three layers for any third-party lender, with compliance and continuity.

Why the orchestration layer wins

  • Lenders don't want to rebuild payroll integrations per provider.
  • Connectivity APIs don't own lender-specific servicing logic.
  • Employer lenders don't serve outside lenders.
  • Nobody wants to rebuild the state-by-state wage deduction compliance matrix.
  • Job changes break every existing solution; continuity is a standalone product.
  • Trust and borrower explanation is a shared liability nobody has owned.

What Kopis is NOT

  • Not a lender — does not originate credit.
  • Not a payroll API — does not own direct payroll integrations where middleware suffices.
  • Not an employer benefits platform — employer distribution is optional, not required.
  • Not a consumer fintech brand — primary buyer is the lender.
  • Not inventing payroll deduction — standardizing how lenders use it.

What Kopis CAN become

  • The default repayment rail any lender ships on top of.
  • The compliance authority of record for voluntary payroll repayment.
  • The continuity engine across job changes.
  • The borrower trust layer that makes paystub line items understandable.
  • The "Plaid for payroll-linked repayment" — one integration, many payroll systems, many lenders.

Legal gameplan — not commentary

Legal is a core product workstream for Kopis — it determines where you can operate, with which payroll providers, for which loan types, at what borrower consent level. Build the legal scaffolding in parallel with the product, not after it.

Patent: where to focus your strategy

Broad claims around payroll deduction won't survive prior-art review — Kashable, BMG, Salary Finance, and the payroll APIs provide decades of documented practice. The good news: the genuinely novel territory is in Kopis's actual product differentiators.

Skip broad claims. File narrow and defensible from day one.

Patent: the defensible angles

  • Employment-change detection and automated re-linking across payroll systems and employers.
  • Compliance rules engine conditioning deduction schedules on jurisdiction, employer, payroll provider, and loan type.
  • Lender-agnostic orchestration abstracting multiple payroll connectivity methods behind one repayment interface.
  • Payment allocation, exception handling, and fallback sequencing when payroll deductions fail or become unavailable.
  • Borrower authorization workflows tied to specific deduction events and servicing changes, with revocation state machines.

Legal workstreams

Patent / prior art / FTO

Freedom-to-operate analysis vs. Kashable, BMG, Pinwheel, Argyle, Atomic, Highline, Salary Finance patents. Narrow claim drafting. Provisional sequencing.

Lending + servicing licensing

Kopis is not a lender but its rails touch loan servicing. Determine whether Kopis is a servicer, a payment processor, a money transmitter, or none — by state and product type.

Payroll provider agreements

Direct integration vs. middleware (Pinwheel/Argyle/Atomic) changes the contract stack. Terms must permit third-party repayment use cases and borrower-authorized data flows.

Borrower authorization

Consent must be informed, specific, revocable, and documented. Consent must cover data access, deduction, employer visibility, and downstream servicing events.

Employer agreements

When employer involvement is required (allotments, non-API payroll systems), you need an enforceable paper trail. Where employer involvement is optional, design it out.

Privacy + data handling

Wage, employer, and paystub data are sensitive. GLBA, state privacy laws, and consumer reporting rules apply depending on data use.

Wage deduction analysis (state-by-state)

Voluntary wage assignments are governed by a patchwork of state statutes. Build the matrix once, version it, and treat it as core IP.

Consumer protection exposure

UDAAP, TILA, FDCPA adjacencies depend on whether Kopis touches origination, servicing, or collection. Clarify role precisely.

Questions counsel must answer

  • In which states is voluntary wage assignment legally recognized for third-party lenders without employer participation?
  • Does Kopis need a money transmitter license in any state given its payment orchestration role?
  • Is Kopis a "servicer" under any state installment loan act?
  • What are the revocation requirements for borrower authorization, and can they be done electronically?
  • Are payroll API middleware ToS permissive for third-party lender repayment use cases?
  • Does any existing patent materially read on the continuity or rules-engine angles?
  • What disclosures are required to the borrower at deduction time, and in what form?

Legal sequencing

  • Provisional patent on narrow orchestration + continuity claims.
  • FTO memo on the eight closest comparators.
  • State-by-state wage deduction matrix, v1 (5 pilot states).
  • Licensing analysis in pilot states.
  • Borrower authorization form + revocation flow drafted.
  • Payroll provider ToS review for pilot middleware.
  • Pilot lender agreement template.
  • Privacy policy and data-handling schedule.

How to read the map

  • Employer lenders prove payroll-deducted lending works, but they cap their TAM at the employer channel and cannot serve outside lenders.

  • Consumer rails like Perpay prove borrowers accept paycheck-linked repayment as a mechanic.

Kopis is adjacent to payroll APIs (may even partner), adjacent-competitive with employer lenders (different buyer), and directly competitive with Highline in the repayment-rail space — with a wider lender-agnostic, multi-vertical, continuity-first wedge.

Accurate positioning builds more credibility than overstatement — and opens partnership conversations with the players you're not competing with.

Product definition

Kopis is a payroll-integrated repayment platform for lenders. It lets a lender integrate once and collect installment repayments directly from borrower wages across many payroll systems, with borrower consent, state-aware compliance, job-switch continuity, and reconciliation baked in.

Product layers

1. Lender-facing API + orchestration

Single integration point. Lender creates a repayment plan, Kopis executes it across whichever payroll connectivity path applies.

2. Payroll connectivity layer

Abstracts middleware (Pinwheel / Argyle / Atomic) and direct payroll integrations behind one interface. Connectivity is rented where it makes sense.

3. Compliance rules engine

State, employer, payroll provider, and loan-type rules encoded as first-class data. Every deduction passes the engine.

4. Borrower authorization system

Informed consent, scope, revocation, audit trail. Consent travels with every deduction event.

5. Servicing + reconciliation

Deduction scheduling, partial payments, NSF analogues, retries, payoff calculations, lender statements.

6. Exception handling

What happens when payroll is late, reduced, paused, or unreachable. Fallback waterfall is a product, not an edge case.

7. Job-switch continuity engine

Detect employment change, notify borrower, re-authorize, re-link at new employer, resume without losing the loan.

8. Borrower dashboard / trust layer

Transparent view of upcoming deductions, pay-stub explainer, revocation, job-change status, support.

MVP scope — build

  • Lender API for plan creation, consent intake, and deduction scheduling.
  • Middleware-based connectivity for one or two payroll providers (via Pinwheel / Argyle / Atomic).
  • Compliance rules engine seeded with 5 pilot states.
  • Borrower authorization flow with explicit scope + revocation.
  • Basic servicing and reconciliation dashboards for the lender.
  • Job-change detection v1 — notify borrower, pause loan, manual re-link.
  • Minimal borrower trust center with pay-stub explainer + FAQ.

Not in v1 — explicitly deferred

  • Direct payroll integrations (let middleware do this at first).
  • Mortgage vertical (operational complexity).
  • Multi-lender payment waterfall prioritization.
  • Automated re-link across employers (manual for pilot).
  • Employer-facing portal (not the buyer in v1).
  • Fifty-state compliance matrix (five is enough for pilot).
  • White-labeled borrower dashboard.

Build vs buy decision

Phase one: buy connectivity, build orchestration. Using Pinwheel or Argyle as the connectivity backbone gets Kopis to pilot dramatically faster than building direct payroll integrations, and keeps capital focused on the layers nobody else has productized: compliance, servicing, continuity, trust.

Phase two: selectively build direct integrations where middleware coverage is weak, where unit economics demand it, or where lender requirements exceed what middleware offers.

Treat connectivity as commodity; treat orchestration as the moat.

Product risks

  • Payroll middleware TOS may restrict third-party lender repayment use cases. Verify in writing.
  • State wage-deduction statutes vary enough to stall expansion; underestimate at your peril.
  • Job-change continuity is a hard problem; if you can't solve it, the pitch weakens.
  • Borrower trust can collapse from a single bad paystub line item showing up on Reddit.
  • Reconciliation errors are existential for lender trust.
  • Competitors may acquire payroll middleware and close the partnership path.

Product principles

  • The borrower is the customer of the explanation, even though the lender is the customer of the product.
  • No silent magic. Every deduction event is explained, visible, and reversible within the consent scope.
  • Compliance is a feature, not a blocker. Make it visible in the UI so lenders see what Kopis protects them from.
  • Continuity is first-class. Job changes are not edge cases; they are a core user story.
  • Rails not funnels. Kopis does not compete with lenders for borrowers.
  • Sober by default. Institutional tone, numerical clarity, no whimsy.

Pilot execution plan — concrete

The pilot is the first real proof of the thesis. Everything about it must be designed to generate one defensible number: the loss-rate delta between payroll-linked repayment and the lender's historical baseline, on a comparable cohort.

Best first pilot: mid-market personal installment lender, 5 pilot states, Pinwheel or Argyle middleware, 6-month pilot, 500–1,500 borrowers opt-in, comparable baseline cohort held constant.

Why personal + medical first

  • Short duration = fast pilot proof (3–9 months of data).
  • State compliance is tractable in a 5-state scope.
  • Borrowers see the benefit immediately — improves opt-in rates.
  • Lender loss-rate baseline is well-documented and easy to compare against.
  • Lower regulatory complexity means fewer blockers if you hit an edge case.

Mortgage is a strong Phase 3 target — loan sizes are compelling — but duration, escrow entanglement, and RESPA complexity make it the wrong place to generate first-pilot proof.

Pilot structure

State scoping

Start with 5 states where voluntary wage deduction is clearly permitted for non-employer-originated loans. Counsel picks from TX, FL, GA, AZ, NC, OH, TN, MO, CO, IN (indicative list, requires verification).

Borrower cohort

Only borrowers in scope states, only those with middleware-supported payroll providers, only those who pass borrower authorization. Target 500–1,500 opt-ins.

Timeline

30 days legal/compliance freeze. 60 days engineering build. 90 days onboarding. 6 months live. 1 month analysis and writeup.

Required metrics

Default rate delta vs. baseline cohort. 30/60/90-day delinquency rate. Collection cost per loan. Opt-in conversion. Revocation rate. Job-change event rate. Exception rate. NPS at month 3 and month 6.

Success criteria

≥30% reduction in 60+ day delinquency. <5% revocation. <10% exception rate. Pilot lender commits to commercial contract.

Failure modes

Opt-in too low (<20%). Middleware coverage gaps. State rules block deduction. Paystub confusion triggers borrower complaints. Reconciliation breaks lender trust.

What the pilot must generate

  • A defensible loss-rate delta and methodology writeup.
  • A borrower experience case study — screenshots, paystub examples, revocation stories.
  • A compliance playbook for the 5 pilot states, verified by counsel.
  • A reconciliation and exception-handling postmortem.
  • A job-change continuity story — at least 10 real events handled successfully.
  • A commercial ROI worksheet investors can redline.

Go-to-market playbook

Kopis sells to lenders. Borrowers and employers are downstream beneficiaries, not the buyer. The sales motion must be B2B, ROI-led, and rooted in loss rate and collection cost economics.

Primary ICPs

  • Mid-market installment lenders ($50M–$500M originations) looking to lower default rates.
  • Medical payment plan providers tied to elective procedures and patient financing.
  • Subprime-adjacent personal lenders where payroll linking materially improves economics.
  • Auto lenders in a second phase — long duration, high collection cost.
  • Specialty SMB lenders where owner payroll access is common.

Best initial customer type

A mid-market personal or medical installment lender with >$100M in annual originations, a visible loss-rate problem, and no in-house payroll integration effort. They have the volume to move unit economics, the pain to justify a pilot, and no sunk cost in a competing rail.

Not: giant banks (too slow), not tiny neobanks (too light on servicing), not employer-benefits lenders (already own the channel differently).

Distribution motion — three options

Lender-driven Recommended

Direct B2B sale to lenders. Kopis is a rail their borrowers opt into. Controls the buyer, preserves neutrality, scales across verticals.

Employer-driven

Sell into benefits channels. Strong trust halo but caps TAM to employers who adopt. Not the primary path.

Borrower-driven

Consumer-facing landing pages for borrowers to route their own loans through Kopis. Too much CAC, weak buying signal. Deprioritize.

Why certain loan verticals come first

  • Personal + medical: shortest duration, simplest compliance, borrower consent easy to obtain, lender ROI fastest to measure.
  • Auto: high value per loan, but TILA-adjacent issues and longer duration make it a phase-two candidate.
  • Mortgage: long duration, enormous servicing complexity, escrow entanglement, RESPA exposure — phase three at earliest.
  • SMB: niche viable if owner-payroll integration is feasible; not the general case.

Brand thesis: institutional, not consumer

Kopis has two audiences with different trust needs. Lenders need to see an institution they can integrate into their servicing stack. Borrowers — who never signed up for Kopis directly — need to see a name that reads as serious, not sketchy, when it appears on their paystub.

Brand rule of thumb: if it looks like a consumer fintech startup, it will lose. If it looks like a bank infrastructure vendor, it will win.

Platform brand (B2B)

  • Sober palette, restrained typography, numerical clarity.
  • Documentation-grade content, not vibes-grade marketing.
  • Lender-facing tone: operations, economics, risk, compliance.
  • Case studies in the register of a payment processor, not a neobank.

Paystub-facing brand (borrower)

  • Clear, pronounceable, Google-friendly name for the line item.
  • Does not imply garnishment, predatory lending, or punitive action.
  • Every paystub label must resolve to a borrower-facing explainer page that ranks for the exact label text.
  • Separation from the B2B brand if needed — some infra companies split this.

Analysis review — claims sharpened

Claim-by-claim review of the original analysis — what holds, what needs sharpening, and the better framing for each.

ClaimStatusContextBetter framing
"No company has built this"RedirectKashable, BMG, TrueConnect, Salary Finance, Pinwheel, Argyle, Atomic, and Highline are all in adjacent or overlapping space.Lead with the gap: "Nobody has productized the lender-neutral orchestration rail." That's accurate and defensible.
Kashable is a 1:1 competitorSharpenKashable is a vertically integrated employer lender — they originate, collect, and own the channel. Kopis is a neutral rail for other lenders."Kashable proves the mechanic. Kopis productizes the rail."
Pinwheel is what Kopis wants to buildSharpenPinwheel is the strongest connectivity layer and a potential partner — not the thing to build. Orchestration, compliance, and lender servicing are the actual product.Position Pinwheel/Argyle/Atomic as inputs Kopis abstracts — not competitors to out-build.
Broad patent claims on payroll deduction will holdSolidPrior art is well-documented across employer lenders and payroll APIs. Broad claims won't survive review.File narrow. Focus on continuity, rules engine, and orchestration — those are the honest IP frontier.
Narrow claims around continuity and rules-engine may be defensibleSolidThese map directly to what Kopis is actually building. This is the right target for counsel.Base the provisional patent strategy here from day one.
Payroll-linked repayment materially lowers default ratesQualifyIndustry evidence from Kashable and TrueConnect supports it — Kopis just doesn't have its own data yet.Cite third-party evidence explicitly. The pilot generates your own proof.
Mortgage is the right first verticalRedirectDuration, escrow entanglement, RESPA, and lender procurement cycles make mortgage a Phase 3 target.Personal and medical installment first. Mortgage is a compelling long-term vertical — just not the pilot.
Employer distribution is the fastest GTM pathSharpenThe trust halo is real, but the TAM is capped and sales cycles run long through HR/benefits channels.Lender-led is primary; employer channel is a secondary trust amplifier.
Highline is a direct competitorSolidClosest repayment-rail comparator in the market right now.Benchmark on neutrality, multi-lender support, compliance depth, and continuity — that's the differentiation surface.
Voluntary wage assignment is broadly legalRedirectIt's a state-by-state patchwork — some states permit it freely, others restrict or ban it.Build the state matrix with counsel. It becomes core infrastructure and genuine IP.
Consumer branding drives lender adoptionRedirectKopis's buyer is the lender. Consumer-facing brand only matters for borrower trust on the paystub line item.B2B institutional brand for lenders; borrower trust center for the consumer touch.
"Plaid for payroll" framingRetireMaps Kopis to a commodity data API and undersells the orchestration, compliance, and servicing layers.Own "lender-agnostic payroll repayment infrastructure." More accurate, more defensible, harder to compare-shop.

What to keep

  • The recognition that broad novelty is weak.
  • The orchestration-layer framing.
  • Narrow patent angles around continuity and rules.
  • The category-map intuition (employer lenders / payroll APIs / repayment rails / orchestration).
  • Sober institutional brand direction.

Framings to replace

  • "Nobody has built this" → lead with the orchestration gap instead.
  • Mortgage as first vertical → save it for Phase 3.
  • Consumer-first branding → institutional B2B brand wins lenders.
  • "Plaid for payroll" → own "lender-agnostic repayment infrastructure."
  • Broad patent claims → file narrow on continuity and rules engine.
  • Blanket legal assumptions → build the state matrix; it's actual IP.
